# Heart Failure Risk Factor Explanations
# Source: NIH Clinical Center, ACC/AHA Guidelines, WHO Reference Ranges
# For demonstration purposes only — not for clinical use

## EJECTION FRACTION EXPLAINED

What it is:
Ejection fraction is the percentage of blood that the left ventricle pumps 
out with each heartbeat. If the ventricle contains 100ml of blood and pumps 
out 55ml, the EF is 55%.

Why it matters in heart failure:
A reduced EF means the heart is not pumping efficiently. The body compensates 
by activating hormonal systems (RAAS, sympathetic nervous system) which 
temporarily maintain blood pressure but ultimately cause further cardiac damage.

Clinical thresholds used in this system:
- EF below 40%: Reduced — qualifies as HFrEF
- EF below 30%: Severely reduced — high risk flag triggered
- EF below 20%: Critical — immediate escalation required

Real data from this patient population:
- Patients who survived averaged EF of 40.3%
- Patients who died averaged EF of 33.5%
- Difference of 6.8% is clinically and statistically significant

## SERUM CREATININE EXPLAINED

What it is:
Creatinine is a waste product produced by muscle metabolism. The kidneys 
filter it from the blood. When kidneys are stressed or failing, creatinine 
accumulates in the blood, causing elevated serum levels.

Why it matters in heart failure:
Poor cardiac output reduces blood flow to the kidneys. Reduced kidney 
perfusion causes acute kidney injury, reflected as rising creatinine.
This creates a vicious cycle — kidney failure worsens heart failure and 
vice versa (cardiorenal syndrome).

Clinical thresholds used in this system:
- Above 1.5 mg/dL: Elevated — kidney stress flag triggered
- Above 2.0 mg/dL: Severely elevated — nephrology consult recommended
- Combined with low EF: Cardiorenal syndrome pattern — HIGH RISK

Real data from this patient population:
- Patients who survived averaged creatinine of 1.18 mg/dL
- Patients who died averaged creatinine of 1.84 mg/dL
- Difference of 0.66 mg/dL is a strong mortality predictor

## SERUM SODIUM EXPLAINED

What it is:
Sodium is an electrolyte that regulates fluid balance in the body.
In heart failure, the body retains fluid (causing edema) and dilutes
sodium concentration, leading to hyponatremia (low sodium).

Why it matters in heart failure:
Low sodium in heart failure reflects neurohormonal activation — the body
is desperately trying to maintain circulation by retaining fluid.
This dilutional hyponatremia is a marker of disease severity.

Clinical thresholds used in this system:
- Below 135 mEq/L: Hyponatremia — fluid imbalance flag triggered
- Below 130 mEq/L: Moderate — active fluid restriction required
- Below 125 mEq/L: Severe — emergency management required

## KIDNEY-HEART RISK INDEX EXPLAINED

What it is:
A composite risk marker created for this system, triggered when BOTH:
- Serum creatinine is above 1.5 mg/dL (kidney stress), AND
- Ejection fraction is below 30% (severe cardiac dysfunction)

Why both together matters:
Either condition alone is serious. Both together indicates bidirectional 
organ failure — the heart and kidneys are failing simultaneously.
This pattern (cardiorenal syndrome) dramatically worsens prognosis.

Real data from this patient population:
- Patients WITHOUT kidney-heart risk: 11.4% mortality rate
- Patients WITH kidney-heart risk: 72.7% mortality rate
- This single composite feature is the strongest predictor in the model

## AGE AND MORTALITY

Clinical significance of age in this population:
- Under 50 years: 23% mortality rate
- 50-60 years: 25% mortality rate  
- 60-70 years: 34% mortality rate
- 70-75 years: 40% mortality rate
- Above 75 years: 61% mortality rate

Age accelerates the risk associated with low EF and kidney stress.
Elderly patients have less physiological reserve to compensate.
The age-creatinine interaction feature captures this combined effect.

## COMORBIDITY SCORE EXPLAINED

What it is:
A simple additive score counting two binary comorbidities:
- Anaemia (1 point if present)
- High blood pressure (1 point if present)
Score range: 0 (neither) to 2 (both present)

Why it matters:
Each additional comorbidity reduces physiological reserve and 
increases the burden on an already compromised heart.
A score of 2 indicates the patient has multiple compounding risk factors.
