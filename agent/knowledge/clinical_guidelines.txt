# Heart Failure Clinical Guidelines
# Source: ACC/AHA 2022 Heart Failure Guidelines, ESC 2021 Guidelines
# For demonstration purposes only — not for clinical use

## EJECTION FRACTION CLASSIFICATION

Ejection fraction (EF) measures the percentage of blood pumped out of the 
left ventricle with each heartbeat. Normal EF is 50-70%.

EF CATEGORIES:
- Normal (>50%): Heart pumping normally
- Mildly reduced (41-49%): HFmrEF — Heart Failure with mildly reduced EF
- Reduced (<40%): HFrEF — Heart Failure with reduced EF
- Severely reduced (<30%): Critical — immediate clinical intervention required
- Critical (<20%): Life-threatening — ICU admission strongly recommended

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
- Every 5% reduction in EF below 40% increases mortality risk by approximately 15%
- EF below 30% combined with elevated creatinine indicates cardiorenal syndrome
- EF below 20% requires urgent specialist escalation regardless of other markers

## SERUM CREATININE AND KIDNEY FUNCTION

Normal serum creatinine: 0.7-1.2 mg/dL (male), 0.5-1.1 mg/dL (female)

CREATININE LEVELS AND CLINICAL MEANING:
- Normal (0.7-1.2 mg/dL): Kidney function adequate
- Mildly elevated (1.2-1.5 mg/dL): Monitor closely, review nephrotoxic medications
- Elevated (1.5-2.0 mg/dL): Significant kidney stress — review diuretic dosing
- Severely elevated (>2.0 mg/dL): Acute kidney injury likely — nephrology consult
- Critical (>3.0 mg/dL): Possible dialysis consideration

CARDIORENAL SYNDROME:
When ejection fraction is below 30% AND creatinine is above 1.5 mg/dL,
cardiorenal syndrome should be suspected. This bidirectional relationship 
between heart and kidney failure significantly worsens prognosis.
Mortality risk increases by 72.7% in patients meeting both criteria.

## SERUM SODIUM AND FLUID BALANCE

Normal serum sodium: 135-145 mEq/L

SODIUM LEVELS AND CLINICAL MEANING:
- Normal (135-145 mEq/L): Fluid balance adequate
- Mild hyponatremia (130-135 mEq/L): Monitor, restrict fluid intake
- Moderate hyponatremia (125-130 mEq/L): Active management required
- Severe hyponatremia (<125 mEq/L): Emergency — risk of cerebral edema
- Critical (<120 mEq/L): ICU admission, IV sodium correction required

HYPONATREMIA IN HEART FAILURE:
Low sodium in heart failure patients indicates neurohormonal activation 
and fluid retention. It is a strong independent predictor of mortality.
Patients with sodium below 135 mEq/L have significantly worse outcomes.

## COMORBIDITY INTERACTIONS

ANAEMIA IN HEART FAILURE:
- Present in 30-50% of heart failure patients
- Worsens symptoms and exercise tolerance
- Associated with increased hospitalization and mortality
- Requires investigation for iron deficiency, renal anaemia

HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE IN HEART FAILURE:
- Chronic hypertension is a leading cause of heart failure
- In acute decompensation, elevated BP increases afterload
- Target BP: less than 130/80 mmHg in heart failure patients

DIABETES IN HEART FAILURE:
- Diabetes present in 30-40% of heart failure patients
- SGLT2 inhibitors now recommended regardless of diabetes status
- Tight glucose control reduces hospitalization risk

SMOKING:
- Active smoking worsens cardiac function and exercise tolerance
- Smoking cessation is a Class I recommendation in all heart failure patients

## MORTALITY RISK STRATIFICATION

LOW RISK:
- EF above 40%
- Creatinine below 1.2 mg/dL
- Sodium above 135 mEq/L
- No kidney-heart risk pattern
- Estimated mortality probability: below 20%

MEDIUM RISK:
- EF 30-40%
- Creatinine 1.2-1.5 mg/dL
- Sodium 130-135 mEq/L
- One or two comorbidities
- Estimated mortality probability: 20-50%

HIGH RISK:
- EF below 30%
- Creatinine above 1.5 mg/dL
- Sodium below 130 mEq/L
- Kidney-heart risk pattern present
- Estimated mortality probability: above 50%

CRITICAL:
- EF below 20%
- Creatinine above 2.0 mg/dL
- Sodium below 125 mEq/L
- Multiple organ involvement
- Estimated mortality probability: above 80%
